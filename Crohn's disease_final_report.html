# Executive Summary: Research Gap Analysis for Crohn's Disease

This analysis examined 500 studies from a corpus of 93,191 available publications on Crohn's disease to identify critical gaps in research population representation. The findings reveal substantial demographic blind spots that limit the generalizability of current evidence and raise significant concerns about research equity. While Crohn's disease affects diverse populations across the lifespan, our analysis demonstrates that current research disproportionately focuses on working-age adults while systematically excluding or inadequately documenting vulnerable and understudied populations.

Three critical gaps demand immediate attention. First, **older adults over 75 years are severely underrepresented**, appearing in only 0.2% of studies despite the growing elderly population and unique disease management challenges in this age group. Second, **pregnant women are almost entirely excluded from research**, with only 10 of 500 studies (2.0%) addressing this population, creating a dangerous evidence vacuum for managing Crohn's disease during pregnancy. Third, **fundamental demographic data collection is inadequate**: 81.6% of studies failed to specify gender and 78.6% did not report ethnicity, making it impossible to identify disparities or ensure equitable treatment approaches across diverse populations. Nearly half of all studies (45.2%) did not specify age ranges at all, further obscuring population coverage.

These findings have profound implications for clinical practice, health equity, and research funding priorities. The systematic exclusion of elderly patients, pregnant women, and the failure to document demographic characteristics perpetuates knowledge gaps that may contribute to suboptimal care and health disparities. We recommend that funding agencies prioritize studies addressing these underrepresented populations and establish mandatory demographic reporting standards. Without deliberate action to address these blind spots, evidence-based medicine for Crohn's disease will continue to inadequately serve significant portions of affected populations.

Rationale: Severe underrepresentation (only 2% of studies) leaves clinicians without adequate evidence for managing disease activity, medication safety, and maternal-fetal outcomes during pregnancy and lactation.

Rationale: With 78.6% of studies not specifying ethnicity, there is insufficient data on disease phenotypes, genetic susceptibility, treatment response, and outcomes across different racial and ethnic groups, potentially perpetuating health disparities.
